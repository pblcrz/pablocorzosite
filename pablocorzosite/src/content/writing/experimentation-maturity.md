Every experimentation maturity model I have seen measures the wrong axis. They count tests. Test count tells you a team has a tool and permission to use it. It tells you almost nothing about whether the organization can act on a result.

This paper proposes a five-stage model along a different axis: the distance between a result and a decision.

## The volume trap

Test volume rises quickly once a platform is in place, because running a test becomes cheaper than arguing. This is genuinely good, and it produces a specific pathology: a large library of underpowered tests, each individually inconclusive, collectively interpreted as evidence.

> Pre-commitment is cheap before the data arrives and impossible after.

## The transition that matters

Moving from stage three to stage four is not a methods upgrade. Stage three organizations often have excellent statisticians. What they lack is a rule, written before launch, that says what happens at each possible outcome and who is bound by it.
