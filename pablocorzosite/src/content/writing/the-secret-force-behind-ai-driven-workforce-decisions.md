Everyone talks about AI replacing jobs. But I wonder if we're missing something bigger.

Over the last two years, AI adoption has spread faster than governance. Employees are using AI to write, code, analyze, research, automate, and create. Teams are purchasing AI tools and departments are building agents and workflows. Productivity is increasing across the organization.

But in many companies, AI spending remains largely invisible and unpredictable, with many seeing the output and few looking at the bill.

This raises an interesting question: What happens when finance finally connects the dots? What happens when organizations realize the question was never just productivity, but productivity economics?

The popular narrative is that jobs will disappear because AI becomes capable enough to do the work. That may certainly be part of the story, but there may be another force quietly building beneath the surface.

Organizations may discover that the cost of (ungoverned) AI isn't concentrated in a single platform or initiative. It's distributed across thousands of prompts, workflows, agents, subscriptions, and automated decisions. Individually, each expense appears negligible. Collectively, they can become a material operating cost that remains largely invisible until leaders begin examining the P&L.

If that happens, workforce reductions may not be driven solely by capability replacement. They may also reflect the difficult reality of leadership teams attempting to rebalance a cost structure that now includes a rapidly growing and often poorly governed AI expense category.

That's a very different conversation.



> The organizations that 'win' won't be the ones that adopt AI the fastest. They'll be the ones that understand its true cost before the bill arrives.



All of which leads me to wonder:**Will AI replace people because of its capabilities, or because of its economics?**
